Project description

In this project, I scraped music data from Billboard Charts. The Billboard Charts calculate the weekly popularity of all songs and albums released in the United States. These metrics are derived from a combination of sources, including the number of singles sales, digital downloads, and a wide array of streaming vehicles (i.e. such as youtube and Spotify). I will primarily investigate the Billboard year-end Hot 100 charts, comparing it against the Digital Sales, Streams, Radio and Album charts that Billboard have also provided. My data analysis will primarily be centred on the below questions:

  • Is there a correlation between singles success and album popularity?
  • Which artists were most dominant across the decade?
  • Who were the up & coming artists in the 2010s?
  • How heavily weighted are digital sales, radio success and streams in the calculation of position on the hot 100 charts?

Rationale behind implementing a point system

When I scraped the data, I noticed there were no discernible values that told much about the positioning of certain items in terms of its rank. Therefore, I decided to implement a point system that allocated different points for each rank within the charts. For example, in the Hot 100 charts, rank no.1 would earn a total of 100 points, whilst rank no.100 would earn a total of 1 point.

Correcting the scale

However, all the Billboard Charts that I scraped were of different scales, which made it difficult to compare the different charts. In the tabs, you will see that I scaled all the charts in accordance with the Hot 100 chart to create a range of points between 100 to 1. For example, to scale the Billboard Streaming, Radio and Digital Sales data, I divided the points by 0.75 as it is three-quarters the scale of the Billboard Hot 100 charts.

Scraping strategy

As I wanted to scrape through years of data, I needed to implement a scraping strategy whereby I could reduce time without having to create a tibble for every single year. After carefully analysing the Billboard web pages for each separate chart category, I realised the only element that changed within the URL was the year. I then created a function (with “year” being the argument) and used the stringr package to add the year to the URL when bowing to gain permission for scraping. I then used the Selector Gadget tool to scrape through different variables within the charts (i.e. Rank, Title, Artist & Album) and created a tibble for each respective chart.

top_albums <- function(year){
  
  rank <- bow(
       str_c("https://www.billboard.com/charts/year-end/", year, "/top-billboard-200-albums")
       ) %>% 
    scrape() %>% 
    html_nodes(".ye-chart-item__rank") %>% 
    html_text() %>% 
    as.numeric
  
  albums <- bow(
    str_c("https://www.billboard.com/charts/year-end/", year, "/top-billboard-200-albums")
    ) %>% 
    scrape() %>% 
    html_nodes(".ye-chart-item__title") %>% 
    html_text() %>% 
    str_remove_all("\\n")
  
  artists <- bow(
    str_c("https://www.billboard.com/charts/year-end/", year, "/top-billboard-200-albums")
    ) %>% 
    scrape() %>% 
    html_nodes(".ye-chart-item__artist") %>% 
    html_text() %>% 
    str_remove_all("\\n")
  
  tibble(Rank = rank,
         Album = albums, 
         Artist = artists,
         Year = year,
         Points = (201 - Rank)/2)
}

Rationale behind analysing solo success

When I scraped the music data from the Billboard Charts, I noticed there were many artists who had duets or collaborations that were equally as popular compared to their solo songs. For ease of the project, I elected to analyse artists who sung solos or who otherwise have frequently collaborated with the same artists over time. Therefore, there is some bias in the dataset in that it does not accurately reflect an artist’s overall popularity in terms of combined single and collaborative success. However, the data does paint an interesting portrait as to the most popular artists on a solo basis.

Data tidying

In determining the top 25 artists across the 2010s for each Billboard category, I created a simple function that collated all the points an artist had accumulated over time. I then ranked the artist according to the total points they earned, filtered the top 25 artists, and subsequently created a column graph that positioned the artists in terms of total points earned.

Conclusions

  • Among all the artists who made the top 25 charts, Taylor Swift, Drake and Bruno Mars consistently performed well over the 2010s, possibly highlighting a growing preference for pop/rap style of music.

  • Interestingly, soundtracks appeared to be the most popular type of album over the 2010s. This is an area of interest that I would like to focus on in the near future!

  • Some key differences were also observed between the Top 25 charts for the Billboard Hot 100 and Albums data. For example, Luke Bryan scored quite high on the Top 25 albums chart, but nevertheless failed to make it into the Top 25 chart for the Hot 100s. This raised some key questions as to whether Hot 100 success was really correlated to album success.

top_25_2010s <- function(billboard){

  billboard %>% 
  group_by(Artist) %>%
  summarise_at(vars(Points),
               list(Points = sum)) %>% 
  arrange(desc(Points))

  total_points %>%
  mutate(Rank = 1:nrow(total_points)) %>% 
  filter(Rank <= 25)

       aes(x = Points,
           y = reorder(Artist, Points)
           )) + 
geom_col(fill = "steelblue4") +
labs(x = "Points", y = "Artist")

}

  4. After completing all the above steps, I was able to create two ggplot graphs, naming the y-variable as the year and the x-variable as either Points or cum_Points in the aesthetic mapping, grouping by each artist to create a facet format.

annual_score <- function(billboard){ 
  
points <- 
  billboard %>% 
  group_by(Artist, Year) %>% 
  summarise_at(vars(Points),
               list(Points = sum)) %>% 
  arrange(Year,
          desc(Points)) %>% 
  group_by(Year)

}

 t10_artists_h100_wide <- 
  annual_score(hot_100) %>% 
  pivot_wider(names_from = "Year", values_from = "Points") %>% 
  replace(is.na(.), 0) %>% 
  mutate(Aggregate = rowSums(.[2:11])) %>% 
  arrange(desc(Aggregate)) %>% 
  top_n(10)

t10_artists_h100_long <- 
  t10_artists_h100_wide %>% 
  pivot_longer(cols = -Artist, 
               names_to = "Year", 
               values_to = "Points") %>% 
  filter(!str_detect(Year, "Aggregate")) %>% 
  group_by(Artist) %>% 
  mutate(cum_Points = cumsum(Points))

Insights

Background

Whilst I wanted to run a regression of the Hot 100 data against the albums data, I was unable to do so as the Hot 100 data did not specify the albums that each song belonged to. However, this gave an opportunity to run a different regression between the Hot 100s data to the streaming, digital sales and radio data, shedding some insight as to how technology has shaped the way we listen to music.

Thus, the aim of the regression was to investigate whether a relationship existed between the position of a song on one chart (i.e. radio, streams or digital sales) against its position on the Hot 100.

Data tidying

  1. To run the regression, I needed to find a way to compare the score that each artist earned for each of their songs within the different Billboard Charts.

  2. I created a function that joined one data set at a time, joining by the “Title”, “Artist”, and “Year”, and renamed the “Points” columns to their chart titles for ease of comparison. Note that streaming data only became available on the Billboard domain after 2013, which is why I filtered the table to only look at the data from 2013 onward. Finally, I omitted the rank from the three data sets as the position of the songs were no longer relevant anymore; instead how they scored in points and how they compared to the number of points on the Hot 100 was the main factor.

Regression Equation

\[ hot_{100} = 15.5375 + 0.3735_{radio} + 0.3847_{streams} + 0.2079_{digitalsales} \]

Interpretation of Regression Equation

Radio

As a song on the radio chart increases by one point, on average that song is 37.5% more likely to increase by one point on the Hot 100.

Streams

As a song on the streams chart increases by one point, on average that song is 38.5% more likely to increase by one point on the Hot 100.

Digital Sales

As a song on the digital sales chart increases by one point, on average that song is 20.8% more likely to increase by one point on the Hot 100.

Intercept

When a song that is listed on the Hot 100 has no feature on either the radio, stream or digital sales chart, their average number of points is around 15.53.

R^2

86.94 % of the variation on a song’s points in the Hot 100 is explained by the variation on its points in the charts.

Conclusion

It is interesting to note that streams had the highest marginal effect whilst digital sales had the lowest. It can indicate the trend of listening to music has slightly changed over the years from buying a single song or album online for a fee to subscribing to streaming service either for free or a monthly fee to obtain a large collection of songs and albums. It may indicate artists that are looking to capture a larger audience to supply their music via streaming services. It’s also worth noting that radio still has a large presence in how people listen to music and is not in fact outdated as some people may believe!

Function to join the Hot 100, radio, streams & digital sales data

join <- function(hot_100, radio, streams, digital_sales){
  
t1_t2 <- 
  left_join(hot_100, radio, by = c("Title", "Artist", "Year")) %>% 
  rename(Hot_100 = Points.x,
         Radio = Points.y,
         Rank = Rank.x) %>% 
  select(-Rank.y)


t1_t3 <- 
  left_join(t1_t2, streams, by = c("Title", "Artist", "Year")) %>% 
  rename(Rank = Rank.x,
         Streams = Points) %>% 
  select(-Rank.y)

t1_t4 <- 
  left_join(t1_t3, digital_sales, by = c("Title", "Artist", "Year")) %>% 
  rename(Rank = Rank.x,
         Digital_sales = Points) %>% 
  select(-Rank.y)

}

h100_regression <-

  replace(is.na(.), 0) %>% 
  filter(Year >= 2013)
